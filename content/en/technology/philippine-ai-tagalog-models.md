When Miguel Santos, a 26-year-old computer science graduate from DLSU, tried to use a popular US-based AI assistant to draft a legal petition in Filipino last year, it responded in a stilted, textbook Tagalog that a Manila court clerk immediately flagged as machine-generated — a small embarrassment that set off a much larger obsession.

"Every major language model treats Filipino as a second-class language," Santos told The Point News from his cramped co-working space in Poblacion. "They were trained on maybe 0.2% Filipino text. They don't know Taglish. They don't know the code-switching. They don't understand that a 'naman' at the end of a sentence changes its entire emotional register."

Santos is the CEO of **Bituin AI**, one of three Philippine startups that are, with little fanfare and even less venture capital by Silicon Valley standards, attempting to build Tagalog-native large language models — systems trained from the ground up on Filipino text rather than fine-tuned from an English base.

## The Three Contenders

**Bituin AI** (Makati) has assembled a 350-million-parameter model trained on 48 billion tokens of Filipino-language text scraped from blogs, court records, social media, and digitized literature from the National Library. The model, called *Diwata-1*, can draft legal documents, answer customer service queries, and — in a demonstration that impressed this reporter — argue both sides of a Senate hearing transcript with contextually appropriate regional idioms.

**Lakay Labs** (Baguio City) is taking a different approach: instead of a single large model, their *Cordillera* system is a federation of smaller, domain-specific models covering agriculture, healthcare, and education, all fine-tuned for indigenous Philippine languages including Ilocano, Kapampangan, and Pangasinan.

**Sugbo Intelligence** (Cebu City) has focused heavily on Cebuano — spoken by approximately 21 million people — and claims its *Bisaya-GPT* model outperforms all commercial alternatives on standardized Cebuano reading comprehension benchmarks.

## The Funding Gap

All three startups face an uncomfortable reality: the compute required to train frontier models costs tens of millions of dollars, and Philippine venture capital has traditionally been cautious about deep-tech bets with no immediate monetization path.

Bituin AI has raised ₱180 million from a consortium of local VCs and a grant from the Department of Science and Technology (DOST), but Santos acknowledges this is a fraction of what comparable US or Chinese projects command.

"We're being scrappy," he says. "We use JAX instead of PyTorch to squeeze more out of our TPU credits. We built our own data pipeline. We sleep four hours."

## Government Interest

The DOST has included Filipino AI language models in its 2026–2030 National AI Roadmap, and the Department of Information and Communications Technology (DICT) has announced a ₱500 million competitive grant program for "AI systems that serve Filipino linguistic and cultural contexts." Applications open next month.

"Language is sovereignty," said DICT Secretary Ivan John Uy. "If the AI that governs our lives, advises our courts, and teaches our children only thinks in English, we have ceded something essential."

## What It Means for Filipinos

The practical applications are significant. A Tagalog-native model could power government chatbots that actually help barangay residents navigate bureaucratic processes without needing to switch languages. It could improve medical diagnosis tools in rural health units. It could make the judiciary — which conducts proceedings in English but serves a population that overwhelmingly thinks in Filipino — more accessible.

Whether any of the three startups survives long enough to realize that potential depends on their ability to attract international partnerships without surrendering control — a balance Santos, Lakay Labs' founders, and Sugbo Intelligence's team are all negotiating simultaneously.

"We are not building this so an American company can acquire us and shelve it," Santos says, with the bluntness of someone who has clearly had this conversation before. "We are building this so a farmer in Batangas can talk to his bank in his own language and actually understand what they're telling him."
